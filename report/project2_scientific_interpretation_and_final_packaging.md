# Project 2: Scientific Interpretation and Final Packaging

## Title

Machine-Learning Assisted Candidate Prioritization in Gaia–LAMOST Chemo-Dynamical Feature Space

## Project Context

Project 2 extends the Gaia–LAMOST Galactic Archaeology workflow from physically motivated candidate selection into machine-learning assisted population exploration.

Project 1 built the reproducible Gaia–LAMOST chemo-kinematic pipeline and identified a set of candidate stars with unusual metallicity and kinematic properties. Project 2 asks whether these candidates remain visible under multiple unsupervised representations of the same feature space.

The goal is not to claim a new stellar stream or merger remnant. Instead, the goal is to test whether candidate stars selected from classical diagnostic criteria also receive support from dimensionality reduction, density-based clustering, and cross-method evidence integration.

## Research Question

Can Gaia–LAMOST chemo-dynamical candidates selected in Project 1 be consistently recovered or prioritized across multiple unsupervised machine-learning views of the feature space?

More specifically, Project 2 investigates whether candidate stars show coherent evidence across:

- physically motivated feature-space design
- PCA baseline embeddings
- UMAP nonlinear embeddings
- DBSCAN clustering
- DBSCAN parameter robustness checks
- candidate-level cross-method evidence summaries

Notebook:

```text

```

Output:

```text

```

Project 2 began by defining multiple feature spaces for unsupervised analysis. These include kinematic-only, chemo-only, kinematic plus photometric, and combined chemo-kinematic representations.

This step is important because stellar-population structure may appear differently depending on which physical dimensions are emphasized. A purely kinematic view may highlight velocity outliers, while a chemo-kinematic view may better identify stars that are both dynamically unusual and metal-poor.

DBSCAN was applied to both PCA and UMAP embedding spaces. This provided a density-based clustering view that does not require pre-specifying the number of clusters.

In this project, DBSCAN labels are interpreted cautiously. A DBSCAN cluster is not automatically a physical stellar population. Instead, DBSCAN provides a way to test whether candidates are embedded in dense regions, located near cluster boundaries, or classified as sparse/noise-like points.

DBSCAN results can vary strongly with hyperparameters such as eps and min_samples. M5 therefore tested the stability of clustering outcomes under a small parameter sweep.

This step prevents over-interpreting a single clustering run. Candidate-level conclusions are stronger when they are not dependent on one fragile DBSCAN configuration.

## Scientific Interpretation

Project 2 supports the view that the Project 1 candidate list contains a subset of stars that are consistently interesting across multiple representations of Gaia–LAMOST chemo-dynamical space.

The strongest interpretation is not that these objects form a confirmed stellar stream or merger remnant. The stronger and safer interpretation is that they are evidence-ranked chemo-dynamical outliers suitable for follow-up analysis.

A candidate is more compelling when several of the following are true:

- it is metal-poor relative to the local sample
- it has high tangential or Galactocentric velocity
- it appears separated or edge-like in PCA space
- it appears distinctive in UMAP space
- it is assigned to sparse/noise-like or candidate-rich DBSCAN regions
- it was already highlighted by Project 1 physical diagnostics
- it remains notable under cross-method evidence integration

This makes Project 2 a candidate-prioritization framework rather than a discovery-claim framework.

## Main Conclusion

Project 2 demonstrates that a lightweight machine-learning workflow can be used to organize and prioritize Gaia–LAMOST chemo-dynamical candidates.

The key scientific conclusion is:

```text
The candidate stars should be interpreted as evidence-ranked follow-up targets, not as confirmed members of a newly discovered Galactic substructure.
```

This conclusion is appropriate for the current sample size, feature set, and exploratory nature of the workflow.

## Limitations

Important limitations remain:

- The sample is still limited compared with full Gaia–LAMOST scale.
- Gaia and LAMOST selection functions are not fully modeled.
- PCA and UMAP embeddings are diagnostic tools, not physical phase-space models.
- DBSCAN labels depend on scaling, embedding choice, and hyperparameters.
- Candidate evidence is cross-method but not yet probabilistic.
- No orbital integration has been performed in Project 2.
- No rigorous comparison with known structures such as Gaia–Sausage–Enceladus, Sequoia, Helmi streams, or disk/heated-disk populations has been completed.
- Chemical information is currently limited mostly to LAMOST stellar parameters such as [Fe/H], without detailed abundance-space modeling.

## Relationship to Project 1

Project 1 answered:

```text
Can a reproducible Gaia–LAMOST pipeline identify physically interpretable chemo-kinematic candidate stars?
```

Project 2 answers:

```text
Do those candidate stars remain interesting under multiple unsupervised machine-learning views?
```

Together, Project 1 and Project 2 form a coherent research sequence:

1. Build the Gaia–LAMOST chemo-kinematic pipeline.
2. Select physically motivated candidate stars.
3. Test candidate visibility in machine-learning feature spaces.
4. Rank candidates by cross-method evidence.
5. Prepare for orbital and literature-level comparison.

## Preparation for Project 3

Project 3 should move from embedding-level evidence to physically stronger Galactic archaeology interpretation.

Recommended next steps include:

- compute orbits for candidate stars
- estimate orbital quantities such as eccentricity, energy, angular momentum, apocenter, pericenter, and maximum vertical height
- compare candidates against known Galactic components and merger debris populations
- test whether the strongest Project 2 candidates resemble halo, heated-disk, or accreted-star populations
- build a more formal candidate table suitable for research outreach, RA applications, or a future manuscript-style report
